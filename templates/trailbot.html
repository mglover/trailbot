Because I'm Old Skool, I hike with a flip phone. Super-rugged, insane battery life, but a barely primitive web browser (and no data plan). Great text messaging.

This is 95% fine. But I still find myself in town trying to find wifi or a computer to do things that most people can do with their smartphone.

TrailBot is learning to do those things.

TrailBot knows how to:

  • fetch a weather report,
  • give turn-by-turn driving directions,
  • manage those group messages you send to friends and family on the road.

You can text TrailBot at:

681-540-1024

First Things First

The first word of your message is the <command>. If you text TrailBot the <command> 'help', like this: help TrailBot will say: This is TrailBot. I understand these <command>s:
help, wx, status, sub, unsub, reg, unreg.
If you know someone's @handle, you can send them a message by starting the message with their @handle
To view the full documentation, visit oldskooltrailgoods.com/trailbot

Locations

TrailBot knows how to take descriptions of places on Earth (sorry @MajorTom) and figure out their exact locations.

The <command>: 'where' tells TrailBot to find a location, like this: where empire state building

And TrailBot will respond: Location Empire State Building
(full name: Empire State Building, 350, 5th Avenue, Manhattan Community Board 5, Manhattan, New York County, City of New York, New York, 10018, United States)
has coordinates 40.74844205 -73.98565890160751

TrailBot (mostly) uses Nominatim, a free geolocation service covering the entire world.

TrailBot can look up these location formats on its own: City, ST (don't forget the comma!): where Washington, DC, latitude/longitude pairs (no comma!): where 38.9413 -78.1911 ZIP codes: where 83843 and, back to it's roots, AT shelternames (with the trail:at hint): where trail:at Low Gap

here and there

If you're using TrailBot, it's likely that you're somewhere on the surface of Earth, and a good chance that you're going somewhere else.

The <command>s 'here' and 'there' can keep track of those places

'here' tells TrailBot where you are, like this: here Leadville, CO

'there' tells TrailBot where you're going: there Jackson, WY

whereupon TrailBot will respond to the first request like this: Success. 'here' is set to: leadville (full name: Leadville, Lake County, Colorado, 80461, United States) coordinates: 39.2508229 -106.2925238 To forget 'here', say 'forget here'

Now you can use 'here' and 'there' as locations. So: where here will tell you that you're in Leadville.

Even better,weather reports and driving directions will use 'here' and 'there' as you'd expect, so wx returns the weather for Leadville, wx there gives you the forecast in Jackson, and drive

tells you how to get there.

'here' and 'there' are shortcuts for 'addr here' and 'addr there', and otherwise work just like any other saved address in your...

Address Book

TrailBot can keep track of more than just here or there.

The <command> 'addr' can give a name to any location

Say 'addr name location', like this: addr vacation fisherman's wharf, san francisco

And TrailBot will reply: Success. 'vacation' is set to:
fisherman's wharf, san francisco
(full name: Fisherman's Wharf of San Francisco, Taylor Street, Port of San Francisco, San Francisco, CAL Fire Northern Region, California, 94133, United States)
coordinates: 37.808332 -122.415715

To forget 'vacation', just 'forget vacation'

After that, you can use the word 'vacation' anywhere that TrailBot expects a location. Like this: wx vacation

And TrailBot will tell you:
2 Miles N San Francisco CA
4 Jun 22:06 pm PDT
Tonight: Mostly cloudy, with a low ~54. SW wind ~17 mph, with gusts as high as 22 mph.

Mon: Partly sunny, with a high near 59. Breezy, with a SW wind 17-26 mph, with gusts as high as 32 mph. [...]

Shared Locations

The <command>s 'share' and 'unshare' let you control who else can see locations in your address book. Locations are private unless you explicitly share them, like this: share vacation with @GroundControl and TrailBot says: Success. Shared vacation with @GroundControl

@GroundControl can now ask: where @MajorTom.vacation and get back: Fisherman's Wharf, San Francisco (full name: Fisherman's Wharf of San Francisco, Taylor Street, Port of San Francisco, San Francisco, CAL Fire Northern Region, California, 94133, United States) coordinates: 37.808332 -122.415715

You can share a location with as many users as you want, or you can share a location with everyone, like this: share vacation

To stop sharing, tell TrailBot: unshare vacation to stop sharing with everyone.

Secret Menu: sharing 'here'

If @MajorTom tells TrailBot: share here

anyone can ask: where @MajorTom and TrailBot will tell them: Cape Canaveral (full name: Cape Canaveral, Brevard County, Florida, United States) coordinates: 28.45077 -80.52662

Weather Reports

The National Weather Service (NWS) providess 7 day weather forecasts in a machine readable format. Not long after I started the AT, I was tired of having to find other hikers to ask for the weather forecast. Spending a few days off-trail to visit my brother, I hooked together the list of AT shelter locations with the NWS website. This is the first thing TrailBot learned how to do.

The <command> for weather reports is 'wx'

'wx' location like this: wx Silver City, NM TrailBot will look up that location, request a report from the National Weather Service, and send you a condensed 3 day forecast: Silver City, NM
30 May 19:15 pm MDT
Tonight: partly cloudy, with a low ~55. SW wend 7-17 mpg becoming NW after midnight. Winds could cust as hight as 23 mph.

Wed: Sunny, with a hight near 78. N NWt wind 6-11 mph SW 16-21 mph in the morning. Winds could gust as high as 26 mph. And so on for two more days.

Driving Directions (BETA)

The Open Source Routing Machine (OSRM) offers a free service providing road and trail routing instructions. TrailBot uses OSRM to give turn-by-turn directions for any two locations it knows about.

The <command> for driving directions is 'drive'

Send 'drive locaction to location', like this: drive Tulsa, OK to Denver, CO

TrailBot will think for a moment, and respond: Turn directions courtesy OSRM

Status Updates

Why?

Without TrailBot, there are two ways I can send a group texts from my phone: send an MMS group message, but then everyone on the list sees everyone else's responses to my updates (and their phone numbers), or send a plain SMS text message to each member of group individually. I like people's privacy too much for the first one, and the second can take a long time and be unreliable, especially as groups get bigger.

Posting Status Updates

To post updates on TrailBot, you have to first register a @handle: reg @MajorTom TrailBot will say: Success: @MajorTom registered" Now you can post your first update: status I'm stepping through the door TrailBot will say: Success: update sent to 0 followers NOTE: status updates are limited to 460 characters in length

Now, people can ask TrailBot about your latest status: status @MajorTom and TrailBot will say: @MajorTom: I'm stepping through the door Or, most usefully, people can subscribe to receive your updates

Subscribing to Updates

If you know someone's @handle, you can subscribe to their updates: sub @MajorTom TrailBot will say: Success: subscribed to @MajorTom. Unsubscribe at any time by sending 'unsub @MajorTom' Now whenever MajorTom posts an update, you'll get a message like this: @MajorTom: I'm floating in the most peculiar way And like TrailBot says, you can stop receiving updates at any time: unsub @MajorTom TrailBot will say: Success: unsubscribed from @MajorTom

Direct Messages

To send direct messages to someone, you need to register a @handle: reg @GroundControl now you can use their @handle to send them a message or reply to their update: @MajorTom Take yr protein pills and put yr helmet on! and @MajorTom will receive: @GroundControl:Take yr protein pills and put yr helmet on!

Handles and registering

A TrailBot @handle is a short, unique name that gets attached to any messages you send, just like on your favorite social media platform. Each @handle can belong to only one phone number, and each phone number can have only one @handle.

TrailBot @handles are limited to 16 characters, letters and numbers only . TrailBot will use whatever Capitalization you register with, but differently capitalized versions of your handle still belong to you. If someone else tries: reg @maJorTom TrailBot will say: Handle '@maJorTom' already exists But if they send: status @MajOrTom TrailBot will say: @MajorTom: Planet Earth is Blue

When you register a @handle, TrailBot will remember your phone number, your last status update and the phone numbers of your subscribers.

You can always unregister your @handle: unreg @MajorTom TrailBot will immediately delete all of the information it has about you and say: Success: @MajorTom unregistered (Twilio, the service that provides TrailBot's connection to the phone network, keeps logs of all messages for [a long time]. Text messages are not private, and there's nothing I can do...)

The 460 character limit

SMS messages are sent in batches of 160 characters, known as a segments. This is why Twitter had their 140 character limit for a long time: they started out as a group text messaging app. Yes, I copied their idea.

460 characters is three segments (160*3=480) minus space for an '@', a 16-character handle, a colon. a space, and one character left over for good luck.

TrailBot is free to use, nicely. Don't abuse the service, or use the service to abuse others. If you're not sure if you're being abusive, ask. If you think someone else is being abusive, let me know. If you like and use TrailBot, consider making a donation.
